# Problem Framing & Positioning Skill

Helps product managers articulate problems from the customer's perspective and create compelling positioning statements using Geoffrey Moore's framework from "Crossing the Chasm."

## When to Use

- Defining the problem before jumping to solutions
- Creating alignment with stakeholders on what problem to solve
- Developing positioning for marketing and sales
- Differentiating from competitors
- Pitching to executives or investors

## Problem Framing Canvas

Frame the problem from the persona's perspective, emphasizing "who" and "why":

```markdown
## Problem Framing Canvas

### Problem Framing Narrative

**I am**: [Describe the key persona experiencing the problem]
- [Key pain point about their underserved need 1]
- [Key pain point about their underserved need 2]
- [Key pain point about their underserved need 3]

**Trying to**:
- [Single sentence listing desired outcomes the persona cares most about]

**But**:
- [Barriers preventing the persona from achieving outcomes]
- [Job-to-be-done obstruction 1]
- [Job-to-be-done obstruction 2]
- [Job-to-be-done obstruction 3]

**Because**:
- [Root cause of the problem or barrier, stated empathetically]

**Which makes me feel**:
- [Emotions felt from the persona's perspective]

### Context & Constraints

- [Geographic factors]
- [Technological constraints]
- [Time-based pressures]
- [Demographic considerations]
- [Regulatory requirements]

### Final Problem Statement

[Single, concise statement providing a powerful and empathetic summary narrative to share with stakeholders, customers, engineering, UX, etc.]
```

## Geoffrey Moore Positioning Statement

Create a positioning statement that communicates who, what, and why:

```markdown
## Positioning Statement

### Value Proposition

**For** [target customer/user persona]
- **that need** [statement of the user's underserved need - consider pains, gains, JTBD]
- [product or service name]
- **is a** [definition of product category]
- **that** [statement of benefit to user - focus on outcomes, not features].

### Differentiation Statement

- **Unlike** [primary competitor or competitive arena],
- [product or service name]
- **provides** [how uniquely differentiated - focus on outcomes, not features].
```

## Positioning Validation Questions

When reviewing positioning statements, verify:

1. **Target Specificity**: Is the target customer clearly defined, not "everyone"?
2. **Need Validation**: Is the underserved need validated by research or data?
3. **Category Clarity**: Does the category help or confuse understanding?
4. **Benefit Focus**: Are benefits stated as outcomes, not feature lists?
5. **Differentiation Truth**: Is the differentiation claim defensible and true?
6. **Competitor Accuracy**: Is the competitor comparison fair and relevant?

## Common Problems to Check For

- **Feature Creep**: Positioning focuses on features instead of outcomes
- **Vague Target**: "Small businesses" instead of "solo accountants with 10-50 clients"
- **Weak Differentiation**: "Better" or "easier" without specifics
- **Category Confusion**: Inventing a category no one understands
- **Me-Too Positioning**: Sounds like every competitor

## Problem Statement Validation

Check that problem statements:

- [ ] **Customer Voice**: Written from persona's perspective, not company's
- [ ] **Emotional Resonance**: Includes how the problem makes them feel
- [ ] **Root Cause**: Explains why the problem exists, not just symptoms
- [ ] **Measurable Impact**: Can quantify the cost of the problem
- [ ] **Solvable Scope**: Problem is specific enough to solve
- [ ] **Research Backed**: Based on customer interviews or data

## Workshop Facilitation Tips

When facilitating problem framing sessions:

1. Start with "I am" to build empathy
2. Let stakeholders debate "Trying to" until aligned
3. Use "But" to surface assumptions about barriers
4. Dig into "Because" to find root causes
5. Don't skip "Which makes me feel" - emotions drive decisions
